## Chapter 06: Chapter 6 — Differentiation and Accessibility
*Source: `chapters/06-differentiation-and-accessibility.md`*

## Bridge

Differentiation changes how students access learning. Chapter 7 turns to a related question: how do students know what quality looks like? Rubrics, exemplars, and clear criteria help every student — not just those with access barriers — understand what they are being asked to produce and how their work will be judged. Claude can help draft those criteria, but the same principle applies: the teacher owns what quality means.

## Chapter 07: Chapter 7 — Rubrics, Exemplars, and Criteria
*Source: `chapters/07-rubrics-exemplars-and-criteria.md`*

## Bridge

Rubrics clarify what quality looks like when students are working. Chapter 8 takes up the harder question that AI forces on every teacher: when a student can use Claude to produce the artifact the rubric describes, does the rubric still measure learning? Assessment in the age of Claude requires looking beyond the artifact to the thinking that should have produced it.

## Chapter 08: Chapter 8 — Assessment in the Age of Claude
*Source: `chapters/08-assessment-in-the-age-of-claude.md`*

## Bridge

This chapter closes the book's Act Two. Chapters 2 through 8 have built teacher-facing workflows: from learning outcomes through lesson planning, feedback, differentiation, rubrics, and now assessment redesign. Chapter 9 begins Act Three: the classroom and institutional questions that require going beyond the teacher's workflow to the conditions under which teaching happens — privacy, student data, institutional policy, and the terms on which students engage with AI.

The teacher is still the system. Act Three asks what the system needs to hold.
