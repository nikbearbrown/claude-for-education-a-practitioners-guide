# Chapter 12 — Capstone: A Claude-Supported Unit

**Capability built:** Design a complete unit or module using Claude — from learning outcomes through assessment — with human review gates at every stage and a verification checklist that confirms the teacher, not the tool, made the decisions that matter.

---

## The Scene

You have read eleven chapters. You have learned to protect learning outcomes from prompt inflation, write Claude requests that begin from what students must be able to do, use differentiation without lowering the intellectual target, design feedback that preserves student agency, and build assessment that requires genuine cognitive labor even when students have AI tools available.

Now build the thing.

This chapter is a construction project. By the end of it, you will have produced a complete Claude-supported teaching unit: learning outcomes, lesson sequence, materials, differentiation plan, feedback plan, assessment, student AI-use policy, and a verification checklist that you can actually hand to a colleague and say: here is how this unit was made and here is where human judgment overrode the machine.

That packet is the capstone deliverable. It is also the test of everything this book has argued.

---

## What This Chapter Produces

The capstone deliverable has seven components. Each was introduced in an earlier chapter; this is the first time they appear together, connected by a single teaching purpose.

| Component | What It Is | Chapter Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Learning outcomes | What students will be able to do, not what topics will be covered | Ch 2 |
| Lesson sequence | Activity-by-activity plan with backward-design logic | Ch 4 |
| Materials | Readings, prompts, handouts, and examples — adapted for your learners | Ch 3, Ch 6 |
| Feedback plan | When, how, and from whom students receive feedback, and what they must do with it | Ch 5 |
| Assessment | How you will know students have learned, not just produced something | Ch 8 |
| Student AI-use policy | What students may and may not do with AI tools, stated in teachable terms | Ch 10 |
| Verification checklist | Record of where Claude helped and where human judgment decided | Ch 7, Ch 11 |

These seven components are not independent. They must cohere: the assessment must measure the outcomes; the feedback plan must serve the assessment; the materials must build toward the lessons; the AI-use policy must be consistent with the assessment design. Claude can help draft each component. The coherence check is your job.

## The Worked Example: A Three-Week Argument Writing Unit

Rather than describing a hypothetical, this chapter builds a real unit. You will see every Claude prompt, every output decision, and every place where human review modified or overrode what Claude produced.

**Context:** A first-year college writing course, three weeks, eighteen to twenty-two students, mixed prior writing experience. The discipline is general education — not a specialized major course. The instructor wants students to be able to construct and defend an evidence-based argument in writing.

This is a deliberately general scenario so that you can adapt it to your own discipline, level, and constraints.

---

## Step 1: Draft the Learning Outcomes

**The backward-design principle (Wiggins and McTighe, 2005):** Begin with what you want students to be able to do, then design activities and assessments that produce and reveal that ability. Do not start from activities.

**Claude prompt — outcomes draft:**

> I am designing a three-week argument writing unit for a first-year general education writing course. Students have varied prior writing experience. At the end of this unit, I want students to be able to write a clearly argued, evidence-supported essay on a topic they have researched. Please draft four to six learning outcomes in this format: "By the end of this unit, students will be able to [verb] [object] [condition or standard]." Use strong action verbs (Bloom's taxonomy is a useful frame). Then flag any outcome that might be hard to assess.

**Claude output (condensed):**

> 1. Students will be able to identify a debatable claim and explain what is at stake in the debate.
> 2. Students will be able to locate and evaluate at least three sources for relevance, credibility, and argument.
> 3. Students will be able to construct a logical argument in which reasons are supported by evidence.
> 4. Students will be able to anticipate and address a significant counterargument in writing.
> 5. Students will be able to revise a draft based on specific peer and instructor feedback.
>
> Flag: Outcome 5 (revision) is process evidence — hard to assess from the final draft alone. Consider requiring a revision memo.

**Human review decision:** Keep all five. Add a revision memo to the assessment to make Outcome 5 assessable. This was Claude's suggestion; accepting it required checking that the revision memo fit the unit timeline. It did.

---

## Step 2: Design the Lesson Sequence

**Claude prompt — lesson sequence:**

> I have five learning outcomes for a three-week argument writing unit. I will paste them below. Please design a lesson-by-lesson sequence (nine class meetings, 75 minutes each) using backward design logic: activities should build toward the outcomes, and each meeting should do assessable cognitive work, not just deliver content. Include what students do before, during, and after each class. Flag any meeting where the cognitive work is lower than I probably want.

**Claude output (condensed — three representative meetings):**

> **Meeting 1 — What Is an Argument?**
> Before: Students read two short op-eds with opposing views on the same topic.
> During: Small groups map the claim, reasons, and evidence in each piece. Class compares.
> After: Students write a one-paragraph "argument spine" for one of the op-eds — claim, one reason, one piece of evidence.
>
> **Meeting 4 — Source Evaluation**
> Before: Students bring two sources they have found for their essay topic.
> During: Partner exchange: each student evaluates the other's sources using a credibility checklist.
> After: Students write a brief evaluation paragraph on each source — what it says, why they trust or distrust it, how it fits the argument.
>
> **Meeting 7 — Counterargument Workshop**
> Before: Students draft the counterargument section of their essay.
> During: "Steel man" activity: students must state their opponent's best case, not a weak version.
> After: Students revise the counterargument section based on workshop feedback.
>
> Flag: Meeting 3 (Thesis Drafting) is currently structured as a lecture. You probably want the cognitive work on the students, not on you. Consider replacing the lecture with a structured drafting activity.

**Human review decision:** Accept the flag. Replace Meeting 3 lecture with a guided drafting workshop. This required writing a new in-class handout — a task Claude can help draft but that the instructor must review for accuracy of example and alignment with course norms.

---

## Step 3: Generate Materials

**One material needed:** The source evaluation checklist mentioned in Meeting 4.

**Claude prompt — checklist generation:**

> For a first-year argument writing course, please draft a source evaluation checklist students will use to evaluate sources they find for their argumentative essay. The checklist should have four to six criteria. For each criterion, give a brief explanation of what students are looking for and an example of what a strong source looks like versus a weak one. Keep the language accessible to first-year college students.

**Claude output:** A six-criterion checklist covering author credentials, publication venue, evidence base, date, stance transparency, and consistency with other sources.

**Human review decision:** The instructor reviewed the checklist against the library research guides their institution uses and found that criterion 3 (evidence base) was accurate but that criterion 5 (stance transparency) was framed in a way that could lead students to distrust all advocacy writing. The criterion was rewritten by the instructor to distinguish between transparency about stance and concealed agenda.

This is the verification discipline the book has built toward: Claude generates a plausible, useful draft; the instructor applies domain knowledge and pedagogical context to make it right.

---

## Step 4: Build the Differentiation Plan

**CAST UDL guidelines (CAST, 2018)** frame differentiation as offering multiple means of representation, action and expression, and engagement — not as creating separate tracks for different students.

**Claude prompt — differentiation review:**

> I have a three-week argument writing unit for mixed-experience first-year writers. I will paste the lesson sequence. Please review it from a Universal Design for Learning perspective and identify: (1) where the unit currently depends on a single modality that could be broadened; (2) where students with less prior writing experience might face a cliff rather than a ramp; (3) where students who are already strong writers might need extension. Suggest specific adjustments for each.

**Claude output (condensed):**

> (1) Meeting 6 (full draft workshop) currently asks students to share their drafts verbally in a large group. Students who find public speaking difficult or who are still developing their written English may struggle disproportionately. Consider offering a written comment option alongside verbal.
> (2) Outcome 4 (counterargument) presupposes that students can first identify the strongest opposing view — a sophisticated move. Students with less debate or argumentation experience may need explicit instruction in identifying what the opponent actually believes before they can steelman it. Add a brief scaffold at Meeting 7.
> (3) Strong writers who finish the essay early and want to go deeper: add an optional extension — a "letter to the editor" version of the argument, which requires the same logic compressed into 250 words.

**Human review decision:** Accept all three. The written comment option for Meeting 6 was easy to add. The scaffold for Meeting 7 required the instructor to write a one-page "what does your opponent actually believe?" worksheet. The extension task was added to the assignment as an optional component.

---

## Step 5: Write the Feedback Plan

A feedback plan answers four questions: When does feedback happen? What form does it take? Who gives it? What must the student do with it (Brookhart, 2017)?

**Claude prompt — feedback plan:**

> For this argument writing unit, please draft a feedback plan that answers: When does feedback happen in the sequence? What form does it take (written, verbal, peer, instructor)? Who gives it? What is the student's required response to feedback? The plan should ensure that feedback happens early enough to improve the final essay — not just on the final draft. Flag any moment in the sequence where the student might receive no feedback for more than one week.

**Claude output:** A structured feedback plan with three formal feedback points: a thesis-and-sources check after Meeting 4 (instructor written comment); a full-draft peer workshop at Meeting 6 (peer written comments using a structured guide); an instructor conference in the final week for students who want to revise after the peer workshop.

**Flag from Claude:** "Currently there is no feedback between Meeting 1 and Meeting 4. Students might drift for the first third of the unit without any check. Consider adding a low-stakes 'argument spine' submission at Meeting 2 with quick instructor or TA comment."

**Human review decision:** The argument spine was already an after-class activity at Meeting 1. The instructor added a brief written-comment response to it within 48 hours, turning it into a genuine feedback point. This required rethinking the instructor's comment load — a judgment Claude cannot make — and adjusting the submission deadlines. The instructor accepted the change.

## Step 6: Design the Assessment

The assessment must measure whether students achieved the learning outcomes. It must not be an artifact test — a test of whether they produced a document — but a learning test (Ambrose et al., 2010).

**Claude prompt — assessment design:**

> I have five learning outcomes for my argument writing unit. Please design an assessment that measures each outcome directly. The final essay cannot be the only evidence — at least some outcomes must be measured through process evidence, not just product. Suggest what counts as evidence for each outcome and how you would evaluate it. Then flag any outcome where it would be easy for a student using AI tools to appear to meet the outcome without actually meeting it.

**Claude output (condensed):**

> - Outcome 1 (identify debatable claim): Assessed by the thesis statement and framing paragraph. Flag: easy to produce with AI. Requires an in-class thesis-drafting exercise as corroborating evidence.
> - Outcome 2 (locate and evaluate sources): Assessed by the source evaluation paragraphs submitted after Meeting 4. Flag: lower risk — students must evaluate specific sources they chose.
> - Outcome 3 (construct logical argument): Assessed by the final essay structure. Flag: moderate risk with AI. A brief in-class free-write on the argument logic, submitted without AI, provides corroborating evidence.
> - Outcome 4 (address counterargument): Assessed in the final essay. Flag: high risk with AI — counterargument sections are exactly what AI does well. Require students to identify the counterargument in an in-class activity before writing the essay.
> - Outcome 5 (revise based on feedback): Assessed by the revision memo — students must quote the specific feedback they received and explain what they changed and why.

**Human review decision:** The revision memo was already planned. The in-class thesis-drafting exercise was added at Meeting 3. The in-class free-write on argument logic was added at Meeting 8. Both address the flagged AI risk by requiring corroborating evidence of student thinking. The instructor verified that the in-class activities are genuinely in-class — they cannot be completed outside the room.

This is backward design completed: the assessment now reaches back through the lesson sequence to require the cognitive work at every stage.

## Step 7: Write the Student AI-Use Policy

The policy must be teachable — specific enough that students know what is and is not allowed, honest about the reasoning, and consistent with the assessment design (Chapter 10).

**Claude prompt — AI-use policy draft:**

> For this argument writing unit, please draft a student AI-use policy that: (1) specifies which parts of the unit students may use AI tools to assist with; (2) specifies which parts they may not; (3) explains the reasoning in plain language; (4) states the disclosure requirement; (5) is consistent with the assessment design. The policy should be one page or less. Flag any part of the policy that might be hard to enforce.

**Claude output (condensed):**

> **AI-Use Policy — Argument Writing Unit**
>
> *What AI tools may help you with:*
> Brainstorming argument topics in the early stages. Checking grammar and sentence clarity in your draft before submission. Generating examples you then evaluate and select from.
>
> *What AI tools may not do:*
> Draft your thesis, reasons, or counterargument for you. Locate or evaluate sources on your behalf. Write any part of your revision memo. Complete any in-class activity.
>
> *Why this distinction:*
> The outcomes of this unit are about your ability to construct and defend an argument. If AI constructs the argument, we have no evidence of your learning. The in-class activities and the revision memo are designed specifically to create that evidence.
>
> *Disclosure:*
> If you use AI tools in any permitted way, note it in a brief statement at the end of your essay: what tool you used and for what purpose.
>
> *Flag:* The prohibition on AI-assisted thesis drafting is hard to enforce on out-of-class work. The in-class thesis-drafting exercise (Meeting 3) serves as corroborating evidence. The policy is most credible because the assessment is designed to make AI-generated arguments identifiable through process evidence.

**Human review decision:** The instructor added one line: "If you are unsure whether a specific AI use is permitted, ask before submitting — not after." This is a relational judgment call that Claude flagged implicitly by noting enforcement difficulty. The instructor resolved it by opening the door to student questions, which shifts the culture from surveillance to disclosure.

## The Verification Checklist

Before the unit goes live, you run it through a final check. This is the record of the unit's integrity — where Claude helped and where human judgment decided.

**Unit Verification Checklist:**

**Outcomes**
- [ ] Outcomes specify what students will *do*, not what will be *covered*
- [ ] Each outcome is assessable by at least one piece of evidence in the assessment plan
- [ ] Outcomes were written or reviewed by the instructor, not accepted verbatim from Claude

**Lesson Sequence**
- [ ] Each meeting requires active student cognitive work
- [ ] The sequence builds toward the outcomes (backward design logic verified)
- [ ] No meeting relies solely on lecture or passive content delivery
- [ ] All Claude flags about low cognitive-demand meetings were reviewed and decided on

**Materials**
- [ ] All factual claims in materials are independently verified
- [ ] Materials have been reviewed for accessibility (reading level, modality, language)
- [ ] Materials are consistent with the course's discipline norms

**Differentiation**
- [ ] Unit offers multiple means of expression (not just one modality)
- [ ] Students with less prior experience have a ramp, not a cliff
- [ ] Extension options exist for students who move faster

**Feedback Plan**
- [ ] Feedback occurs early enough in the sequence to improve final work
- [ ] No student goes more than one week without any feedback touchpoint
- [ ] Students are required to respond to feedback, not just receive it

**Assessment**
- [ ] Assessment includes process evidence, not just product
- [ ] Each outcome has at least one piece of assessment evidence
- [ ] AI-risk review was conducted; corroborating in-class evidence exists for high-risk outcomes
- [ ] In-class activities are genuinely in-class

**AI-Use Policy**
- [ ] Policy specifies permitted and prohibited uses
- [ ] Reasoning is stated in plain language
- [ ] Policy is consistent with the assessment design
- [ ] Disclosure requirement is stated
- [ ] Policy was reviewed by the instructor (not accepted verbatim from Claude)

**Privacy and Institutional Compliance**
- [ ] No student data entered into Claude during unit design
- [ ] Unit complies with institutional AI tool policy [verify — check with your institution]
- [ ] Any required AI-use disclosure to department or institution has been made

**Human Judgment Record**
- [ ] List the three most significant decisions in this unit where human judgment overrode or substantially modified Claude output:
  1. _______________
  2. _______________
  3. _______________

That last item is not rhetorical. Fill it in. If you cannot name three decisions where your judgment shaped the unit differently than Claude's draft, you may have delegated more than you intended.

## The AI Wayback Machine: Where This Practice Comes From

**Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe** published *Understanding by Design* in 1998 (Wiggins and McTighe, 2005). Their argument was simple and durable: start with what you want students to be able to do, then design backward to activities and assessments. A quarter-century later, that logic is exactly what prevents AI-assisted unit design from becoming artifact assembly. The backward design discipline is not about Claude; it is about whether the unit is actually designed for learning.

**CAST** developed the Universal Design for Learning framework to ensure that the way a unit is designed does not inadvertently exclude learners through single-modality dependence, assumed prior experience, or inaccessible materials (CAST, 2018). Claude can generate differentiation suggestions, but the discipline to apply them comes from UDL's foundational commitment that access to learning is not optional.

**Susan Brookhart** spent decades researching the conditions under which feedback actually changes student work (Brookhart, 2017). The finding is consistent: feedback must be timely, specific, and accompanied by an opportunity to use it. Claude can draft a feedback plan, but the evidence that feedback matters comes from the research Brookhart and others built — and from the instructor's discipline to make feedback a structural feature of the unit, not an afterthought.

The tools change. The principles do not. What has changed with Claude is the speed at which plausible drafts of all of these components appear. Speed creates a new risk: a unit that looks complete because all seven components exist, but whose components were accepted rather than decided. The verification checklist is the defense against that risk.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating the capstone as a Claude demonstration.** The point of this unit is not to show how much Claude helped. It is to show that you designed a coherent learning experience. If the most interesting thing about your unit is the prompts you used, the design is not finished.

**Accepting coherence from Claude.** Claude will draft seven components that feel like they go together. They may not actually cohere — an AI-use policy that permits thesis drafting in a unit where the assessment cannot detect AI-generated theses, for instance. Coherence is your review job.

**Filing the verification checklist without filling it in.** The checklist is not a formality. The human judgment record is the accountability mechanism.

**Designing a showcase unit rather than a real one.** The capstone is most useful if you design a unit you will actually teach, with your actual students, in your actual context — not an idealized unit that requires conditions you do not have.

## Try This

**Exercise 1 — Build Your Own Packet**

Choose a unit you currently teach or plan to teach — at minimum two weeks, at minimum one major assessment. Work through each of the seven capstone components using the prompts in this chapter as models. At each step, record the decision you made when you reviewed Claude's output. At the end, fill in the three human-judgment decisions in the verification checklist.

**Exercise 2 — Red Team the Unit**

After completing your unit packet, use this prompt:

> I am going to paste a unit plan I have designed for [course, level, duration]. Please read it as a skeptical colleague who is looking for: (1) places where the outcomes and assessment do not align; (2) places where the student AI-use policy conflicts with the assessment design; (3) any outcome that cannot be assessed by the evidence I have described; (4) any place where the cognitive work is lower than I probably want. Be direct. I want to find the problems before my students do.

Review Claude's critique. Decide which concerns are valid and which are artifacts of how you described the unit in the prompt. Make the changes that are genuinely warranted. Do not make changes just because Claude raised them.

## What Would Change My Mind

This capstone assumes that a seven-component unit packet, built with Claude and verified by the instructor, is a responsible and effective approach to Claude-supported teaching design. What would change that judgment?

If evidence accumulated that the verification checklist creates a false sense of security — that instructors check boxes without actually reviewing outputs — the checklist format would need rethinking. A portfolio plus a review memo (as the research for this chapter suggests) might be more robust: rather than a checklist, the instructor writes a brief memo explaining their most significant human-judgment decisions. More labor; more genuine.

If the speed of Claude-assisted design produces units that are more numerous but less carefully taught, the net effect on student learning could be negative. More materials, more activities, less instructor presence — that would be a worse outcome than fewer, better-taught units.

## Still Puzzling

**Does the verification checklist become a compliance ritual?** Any checklist can be reduced to box-checking. The human judgment record is an attempt to make the document non-trivial. Whether it works depends on the instructor's discipline, not the document's design.

**How does this scale to team-taught courses?** A single instructor making three deliberate human-judgment decisions is one thing. A course committee of five instructors using Claude to build a shared curriculum is a different design problem. The coherence check becomes a conversation, not a solo review. This is worth a follow-up chapter this book does not have room for.

**What happens when students see the prompt log?** If you taught this unit and then showed students the prompts you used and the decisions you made, would that increase or decrease their trust in the course? The answer is probably: it would increase it, because the decision log demonstrates that the teacher was present and thinking. That transparency might itself be a teaching move worth considering.

## Closing: The Teacher Is Still the System

This book opened with a sentence: Claude can help teachers prepare, respond, and adapt, but it cannot decide what learning is for or whether a student has truly learned.

The capstone unit is the proof of that sentence. Claude drafted the learning outcomes, the lesson sequence, the materials checklist, the differentiation suggestions, the feedback plan structure, the assessment flags, the AI-use policy, and even this verification checklist. Every draft exists. Every draft was reviewed, modified, and in some places overridden by an instructor who knew the students, the course, the field, and the institution in ways Claude does not and cannot.

The unit that results is not a Claude product. It is a teaching product that Claude helped build. The difference matters — not as a fine point of attribution, but as the design decision that determines whether students actually learn.

Polished artifacts are everywhere now. The gap between a polished artifact and a trustworthy one is not closed by better prompts. It is closed by the instructor who knows what learning is for, who the students are, and what it means to be responsible for the room.

That work has not changed. The tools have changed around it.

Begin.
