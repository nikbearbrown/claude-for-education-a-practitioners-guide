# Chapter 10 — Student Use: From Ban to Boundary

**Capability built:** Write student-facing AI-use rules that are clear, teachable, and aligned to learning goals.

---

## The Scene

A community college composition instructor spent the fall semester adding "no AI tools" to every assignment sheet. By November she had received three student appeals, discovered that her AI-detection software had flagged a student who wrote in English as a second language, and watched a student who genuinely struggled get zero credit on a paper that was largely their own work. By December she had a policy that generated more conflict than it prevented.

She did not have the wrong instinct. She had an unworkable tool.

A blanket ban assumes the problem is access. The problem is not access. Every student with a phone now has access to generative AI. The problem is design: most assignments, as written, do not force students to do the intellectual work the assignment is supposed to require. Banning the tool does not redesign the work. It just adds a rule that is difficult to enforce and teaches nothing.

This chapter is about the alternative — not permissiveness, but structure. It is about moving from "don't use AI" to "here is what using AI looks like in this class, and here is why the boundaries are where they are."

## Why Banning Usually Fails

Banning generative AI in education is not wrong in principle. There are legitimate tasks — building foundational skills, completing in-class work, high-stakes credentialing — where no-AI is a reasonable and enforceable rule. The problem is using a ban as a universal default.

Bans carry three liabilities.

**They rely on detection that does not work reliably.** AI-detection tools generate false positives at rates that are professionally and legally consequential (U.S. Department of Education, 2023). Writers who are not native speakers of English are disproportionately flagged. No detection tool has established the reliability threshold that would justify using its output to accuse, sanction, or fail a student.

**They do not teach anything.** A student who avoids AI to comply with a ban has learned to avoid detection, not to think carefully about when AI is and is not useful. When they graduate and encounter AI in the workplace, they will have no framework for that decision. Teaching the boundary is more durable than enforcing the ban.

**They do not engage the real question.** The real question is not "did a student use AI?" It is "did a student do the thinking the assignment was designed to develop?" Those are different questions with different answers and different pedagogical responses (Wiggins and McTighe, 2005; Ambrose et al., 2010).

## Core Concept: Four Policy Modes

The research on student AI policy suggests that durable, teachable policies distinguish among different types of use, rather than treating all AI use as identical (UNESCO, 2023; U.S. Department of Education, 2023). This chapter uses four policy modes. Every assignment in a course falls into one of them.

**Mode 1 — Prohibited**
No AI tools, full stop. This mode is appropriate when the assignment is explicitly designed to build a foundational skill through unassisted practice, when the assessment is in-class or synchronous, or when the credential or standard being assessed requires unassisted performance. The prohibition should be explicit in the assignment description, and the reason should be stated plainly: "This assignment is practicing [skill]. We need to see your unassisted thinking."

**Mode 2 — Permitted with Disclosure**
Students may use AI tools, but must document what they used, how, and for what purpose. This mode is appropriate when AI is a plausible tool in the discipline and the assignment's intellectual demands survive AI assistance — because the work requires integration, synthesis, judgment, or situational knowledge the tool cannot provide. Disclosure is not punishment. It is professional practice modeled after citation norms. Students learn to account for their tools.

**Mode 3 — Required for Process**
AI use is built into the assignment design. Students must interact with Claude (or another tool) as part of the work — for example, to generate a first draft they then critique, to surface counterarguments they then evaluate, or to test an explanation against Claude's questions. This mode is appropriate when AI literacy itself is a learning outcome. The assignment requires students to demonstrate judgment over AI output, not just production of their own.

**Mode 4 — Reflective Use**
Students use AI at their own discretion during preparation or drafting, then write a structured reflection on how the tool affected their thinking. This mode is appropriate in courses where metacognition and process awareness are explicit learning outcomes. The submission is the student's work plus an honest account of the AI's role. The reflection is assessed, not just the artifact.

## Writing Policy Language That Works

Vague policy language generates confusion and conflict. Specific policy language is teachable and fair. The difference is the level at which the rule is written.

**Too vague:** "AI use is not permitted in this course except where appropriate."

**More specific:** "In this course, AI tools may not be used to generate text you submit as your own writing. AI may be used to brainstorm, to research background information, or to check grammar after you have completed a draft. If you use AI during any of these steps, attach a brief note to your submission describing what you used and for what purpose."

The specific version tells the student:
- What is prohibited (submitting AI-generated text as their own)
- What is permitted (brainstorming, background research, grammar check)
- What is required when they do use AI (a brief note)

It does not rely on detection. It does not assume bad faith. It models professional transparency.

When writing policy language for your course, draft it at this level of specificity. Then ask: could a conscientious student follow this? Could they explain it to a classmate? If not, revise until the answer is yes.

## Worked Walkthrough: Redesigning the Vulnerable Assignment

The composition instructor from the opening scene taught a research-based argument essay. Her students were supposed to practice finding sources, evaluating evidence, and constructing an argument. The original assignment asked students to write a 1,200-word essay on a policy topic of their choice. AI could easily produce that essay, beginning to end.

She did not need to ban AI. She needed to redesign the work so that AI assistance is visible, bounded, and insufficient to complete the assignment without student thinking.

**Redesigned version (Mode 2 — Permitted with Disclosure):**

Week 1: Students submit a 200-word problem statement explaining why the policy topic matters. This is an in-class timed writing exercise. No AI.

Week 2: Students submit an annotated source log — five sources found and evaluated with brief notes on each source's relevance and credibility. Annotation is their own; they may use AI to help locate sources but must document this.

Week 3: Students submit a draft argument with counterargument. If they used AI to help draft, they must attach a log noting what prompts they used and what they changed from the AI output.

Week 4: Students attend a ten-minute instructor conference and explain their argument orally. The conference questions probe the reasoning in the draft.

Week 5: Students submit a final essay with a revision note explaining what changed from draft to final and why.

What changed: The intellectual work is distributed across the assignment. Each stage requires something the student must produce from their own thinking and knowledge. The oral conference in Week 4 is what Chapter 8 called the "oral check" — the moment that reveals whether the student can own the argument they submitted. AI cannot attend that conference for them.

The policy is not "no AI." The policy is "AI is permitted with disclosure, and every stage has a component requiring your own thinking." That is teachable. That is survivable.

## The Human Gate: Explaining the Boundary to Students

Students deserve to know why the boundary is where it is. "Because I said so" or "because it's cheating" are not answers that build AI judgment. An answer that builds judgment sounds like this:

"The reason you can't use AI to write this essay is not that I can detect it. I can't, reliably. The reason is that the goal of this assignment is for you to practice constructing an argument from evidence. If AI does that work, you don't practice it. You won't have it when you need it later. The boundary is where it is because of what you are supposed to be able to do by the end of this course."

This framing does something important. It connects the boundary to the student's own learning rather than to the teacher's enforcement problem. Students who understand why a limit exists are more likely to observe it voluntarily and more capable of reasoning about analogous limits in new situations (Ambrose et al., 2010; Brookhart, 2008).

If your institution has access to this feature, it is worth knowing about: it is an example of a tool boundary being designed into the tool itself, rather than enforced externally (Anthropic, 2024).

## Sample Disclosure Format

Give students a simple format and they will use it. A disclosure requirement that is vague or burdensome will be ignored or gamed.

Here is a usable template:

> **AI Use Disclosure**
>
> Assignment: [Title]
> Submitted by: [Name]
>
> I used the following AI tools in completing this assignment:
> - Tool: [e.g., Claude]
> - Stage of work: [e.g., brainstorming, checking grammar, generating counterarguments]
> - What I changed: [e.g., I used AI to generate three possible thesis framings, then wrote my own based on one of them. I revised the language substantially.]
>
> I did not use AI for: [any stages where AI was prohibited or where you chose not to use it]

This format is modeled on practices from academic integrity guidance and digital-literacy frameworks. It treats AI use as a professional behavior that requires documentation, not a confession that requires defense. Normalize it and most students will fill it out honestly.

## Equity and Access

Any AI policy must reckon with the fact that students do not have equal access to AI tools or equal background in using them effectively. A policy that assumes all students have paid accounts, high-bandwidth internet, and prior AI experience will disadvantage students who do not — students who are often already disadvantaged in other ways.

Two principles follow from this.

First, if you require AI use (Mode 3 or Mode 4), ensure equitable access. If Claude or another tool is required for a course activity, that tool should be available through institutional accounts, library access, or another mechanism that does not place the cost on individual students. UNESCO's guidance on generative AI in education flags access equity as a precondition for responsible AI integration (UNESCO, 2023).

Second, teach the tool, not just the policy. Students who have never used Claude effectively will not use it well just because you assign it. A short in-class activity — exploring what Claude does when you ask it to just give you an answer versus when you ask it to help you think through a question — gives all students a baseline. It also makes the policy real rather than abstract.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Writing a policy, not a pedagogy.**
A rule without explanation is a rule without traction. The policy becomes teachable when it is connected to a learning outcome the student can see. Always say why.

**Mistake 2: Using the same mode for every assignment.**
Different assignments have different intellectual purposes. A timed in-class journal (Mode 1) and a semester-long research project (Mode 2) are not the same thing and should not carry the same AI policy. Match mode to purpose.

**Mistake 3: Relying on detection.**
Detection tools are not reliable at the level of individual student work (U.S. Department of Education, 2023). Building a policy around detection is building on sand. Build instead on assignment design that requires student thinking, and on oral checks or process evidence that reveal it.

**Mistake 4: Treating disclosure as punishment.**
If disclosure is only required for students who are caught, it signals that AI use is shameful. If disclosure is routinely required for AI-assisted work across the class, it normalizes professional transparency. The difference is in how you frame it from day one.

**Mistake 5: Ignoring the equity dimension.**
If AI literacy becomes an unstated prerequisite for course success, students who lack it will be disadvantaged. Build the baseline into the course.

## Try This

**Exercise 1: Four-Mode Audit**

Take three assignments from a course you teach. For each, answer:

1. What is the intellectual skill or outcome this assignment is designed to build?
2. Can AI produce the artifact this assignment requires without that skill being exercised?
3. Which of the four modes (Prohibited, Permitted with Disclosure, Required for Process, Reflective Use) best fits this assignment's purpose?

Revise the assignment description to include a one-sentence AI policy that names the mode and states the reason. Read it aloud. If it sounds arbitrary, revise until it sounds pedagogically grounded.

**Exercise 2: Draft Syllabus Language**

Write a paragraph of syllabus language for a course you teach or support. The paragraph should:

- Name the overall approach to AI tools in the course (not a binary ban or universal permission).
- Give one example of an assignment category where AI is prohibited and explain why.
- Give one example where AI is permitted with disclosure.
- Provide the disclosure format students should use.

Share the paragraph with a colleague and ask: Is this clear enough that a student could follow it? Is the reasoning transparent? Revise based on the response.

## What Would Change My Mind

This chapter takes a position: structured policy is better than blanket prohibition. What evidence would change that?

If robust, validated, individual-specific assessment were routinely available — meaning every student could be assessed at a level of granularity that distinguishes their thinking from AI output, through oral exam, observed practice, or portfolio review — the case for permitting AI in certain assignments gets stronger. The assessment, not the policy, would do the protecting.

Conversely, if research demonstrated that even structured, bounded AI use during foundational skill-building consistently reduces mastery of those skills — not just slows production, but actually impairs long-term competence — the case for tighter restrictions in certain skill-building contexts gets stronger. The evidence on this is still developing. Pay attention to it (Ambrose et al., 2010; Boud and Falchikov, 2007).

What would not change the core principle: the availability of a detection tool, no matter how sophisticated. Detection is not pedagogy. Assessment is.

## Still Puzzling

**What counts as authorship in an AI-mediated workflow?** Academic integrity traditions evolved around a clear notion of authorship: words, ideas, and arguments belong to the person who produced them. AI complicates this, and the field is still working out what intellectual ownership means when a human directs a process but does not write every word. Educators should watch how academic integrity bodies in their disciplines update their guidance — this is moving faster than most institutional policies.

**Does disclosure actually reduce academic dishonesty, or does it just move it underground?** Requiring disclosure depends on students being willing to disclose. Some will not be. The pedagogy argument — that students who understand why a boundary exists are more likely to observe it — is plausible, but it is not yet a well-established empirical claim. Longitudinal research on disclosure-based AI policies in real classrooms would be valuable.

**What happens when student-facing AI tools learn from interactions?** If an AI tutoring tool improves partly by learning from student interactions, what data does it retain, and does that create privacy obligations? This is a place where Chapter 9's concerns about student data and Chapter 10's concerns about student use overlap. The intersection is not yet well-mapped in institutional policy.
