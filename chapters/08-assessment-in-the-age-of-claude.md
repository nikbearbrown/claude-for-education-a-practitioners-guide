# Chapter 8 — Assessment in the Age of Claude

---

## The Assignment Has Already Changed

Before the semester begins, before the syllabus is printed, before any student opens the assignment prompt — something has already changed. The take-home essay that once required a student to sit alone with sources, struggle with an argument, and write sentences they had not seen before can now be drafted in ninety seconds with a prompt to Claude.

This is not a new kind of academic dishonesty. It is a new condition of the assignment.

Dishonesty requires intent. What generative AI introduced is something more structural: an assignment design that no longer reliably measures what it was designed to measure. A take-home essay prompt measures a student's ability to organize and argue in writing — or it measures a student's ability to prompt Claude to do that. From the outside, the two products can look identical. The polished draft is back; the learning it was supposed to evidence may not be.

The practical question is not whether students will use Claude. Some will. Some won't. Some will use it in ways that undermine their learning; others will use it in ways that extend their thinking. The relevant question for assessment is: what does this assignment actually measure, and does the design create evidence that shows student thinking rather than AI output?

This chapter argues that the productive response to Claude is not primarily detection, prohibition, or alarm — it is redesign. Well-designed assessments were always better than their poorly designed counterparts. Claude has made the gap visible.

## Core Concepts

**Assessment validity** means the assessment measures what it claims to measure. An essay prompt that claims to assess a student's ability to construct an argument is not valid if the evidence of argument-construction has been produced by Claude. Wiggins and McTighe's work on backward design puts validity at the center of assessment: start from the evidence you need, then design the task that produces it (Wiggins and McTighe, *Understanding by Design*). Generative AI has not changed this principle; it has made violations of it more consequential.

**Authentic assessment** asks students to apply knowledge in contexts that resemble how that knowledge is actually used. A transfer task — using a concept from the course to analyze a new case the student has not seen — is more authentic than a summary of course material, and substantially harder for AI to substitute without student knowledge. Boud and Falchikov's research on assessment for lifelong learning emphasizes that the most durable assessments are those that require judgment, not retrieval (Boud and Falchikov, assessment and lifelong learning).

**Process evidence** shifts assessment attention from the product to how the product was made. Drafts, annotations, revision logs, recorded decision points, and reflective commentary create a record of student thinking that a polished AI-generated final product does not contain. Royce Sadler's formative assessment research identifies process visibility as central to how students develop standards of quality in their own work (Sadler, formative assessment).

**AI-use disclosure** is the practice of requiring students to report what AI tools they used, how, and for what purpose. It does not prevent misuse; it creates a record that can be checked and a norm of transparency that shapes behavior. UNESCO's guidance on generative AI in education recommends disclosure requirements as part of assessment integrity without treating all AI use as prohibited (UNESCO generative AI guidance).

**Cognitive labor** is the thing assessments are designed to make visible. When Claude handles the cognitive labor and the student submits the output, the assessment has measured Claude. This chapter's underlying concern — which it shares with the rest of this book — is that assessments protect the cognitive labor that learning requires.

## The Vulnerability Map

Not all assessments are equally vulnerable to AI substitution. A rough map:

**High vulnerability:**
- Take-home essays on general or well-documented topics
- Research summaries
- Multiple-choice and short-answer tests given as take-home or untimed work
- Discussion board posts on abstract topics
- Lab or case reports with standard templates

**Moderate vulnerability:**
- Analytical papers on course-specific texts students must interpret
- Design projects with general parameters
- Group projects where individual contribution is unclear

**Low vulnerability:**
- In-class writing or speaking under observation
- Work that requires the student's own data, field notes, or site-specific observations
- Oral examinations or live demonstrations
- Projects requiring continuous process documentation that the student controls
- Transfer tasks applied to novel cases introduced close to the submission date

This map is not a prohibition list for the high-vulnerability column. It is a diagnostic. A take-home essay is not inherently wrong; it becomes problematic when the design allows AI to substitute for the student's thinking without any design feature that makes that substitution visible or difficult.

## Five Assessment Design Patterns

### Pattern 1: The Oral Component

Add a live conversation to any written assessment. After students submit work, meet with them — individually or in small groups — for a five-to-ten-minute conversation about what they wrote. Ask them to explain a claim, walk through their reasoning, identify a counterargument they did not address, or connect their argument to a new case you introduce in the conversation.

A student who engaged seriously with the written work can answer these questions. A student whose written work was produced by Claude without engagement will have difficulty. This is not a gotcha. It is assessment doing what assessment is supposed to do: produce evidence of what the student knows.

The oral component does not have to be formal or high-stakes. "Walk me through your argument" at the start of class, or brief check-ins during a writing workshop, can serve the same function.

### Pattern 2: Process Portfolio

Require process as part of the submission. A process portfolio might include:

- A pre-writing brainstorm or outline showing the student's thinking before drafting
- Two or more drafts with revision notes explaining what the student changed and why
- A brief reflection: What was hard? What did you learn from revising? What would you change if you had more time?

The brainstorm and revision notes are difficult for AI to produce in a way that documents genuine student thinking — particularly if they are dated, specific to the student's actual process, and required before the final draft is submitted. Ambrose et al. note that self-regulation — the ability to monitor and adjust one's own learning — is itself a teachable skill, and process portfolios develop it (Ambrose et al., *How Learning Works*).

### Pattern 3: Transfer Task

Design assessments around cases or data the student has not seen before. If you introduce the novel case close to the submission deadline — or in class on the day of the assessment — AI cannot have been primed to produce an answer. The student's response to the new case draws on learning they have internalized, not on a prompt they submitted earlier.

A biology course might ask students to analyze a research scenario they have not seen, applying concepts from the unit. A history course might assign a document from the week the assessment is due, asking students to situate it using frameworks from the course. A business course might present a brief case study distributed at the start of the final exam period.

Transfer tasks are the most direct measure of understanding. Wiggins and McTighe's evidence-centered design puts transfer at the top of the assessment hierarchy: if students can apply knowledge to new problems, they have learned it (Wiggins and McTighe, *Understanding by Design*).

### Pattern 4: The AI-Use Reflection

Rather than prohibiting AI, require students to report its use and reflect on it. A disclosure prompt might read:

> If you used any AI tool in the preparation of this assignment, describe: which tool, for what purpose, and at what stage of your work. Then write two sentences about what you decided to do differently from what the AI produced, and why.

This prompt does three things. It normalizes disclosure. It requires students to maintain authorial agency over AI use. And it makes the reflection part of the learning — students who articulate why they changed or rejected AI output are doing exactly the kind of critical evaluation the course should be developing.

For students who did not use AI, the disclosure section is a brief statement to that effect. For students who used it well, the reflection demonstrates judgment. For students who used it to substitute for their thinking, the reflection question creates a moment of difficulty they must account for.

This design does not catch every case of misuse. It does not try to. It creates a norm, a record, and a habit. [verify — current as of writing: institutional policies on AI disclosure requirements vary widely; confirm your institution's current position before implementing]

### Pattern 5: In-Class Work

Reserve some portion of high-stakes assessment for in-class conditions. This does not mean only closed-book, high-pressure exams — it means assessment that happens in a space where the teacher can observe the process. In-class writing, collaborative problem-solving, live presentations, and lab work all produce evidence under conditions the teacher can see.

In-class conditions protect the assessment's validity without requiring detection or accusation. A student who has relied entirely on Claude for take-home work will find in-class conditions a different experience. This information is useful for the teacher: it reveals what learning has actually taken place.

## Redesigning a Vulnerable Assessment: A Worked Example

### The original design

A university sociology course assigns a five-page take-home essay:

> Analyze how social media platforms affect adolescent identity formation. Use at least five peer-reviewed sources. Due in two weeks.

This is a general topic, well-documented, with plenty of AI-accessible source material. The assignment is high-vulnerability.

### Step 1: Identify what learning it is meant to measure

The teacher wants students to: apply sociological concepts to a contemporary case; synthesize peer-reviewed literature; construct an original argument supported by evidence.

### Step 2: Identify what AI can substitute

Claude can produce a structurally coherent five-page essay on this topic with synthesized source citations [verify — current as of writing: AI citation accuracy varies; Claude may hallucinate sources]. What Claude cannot authentically substitute: the student's own reading of sources, original synthesis, and live defense of claims.

### Step 3: Redesign using the five patterns

The teacher makes three changes:

1. **Process requirement:** Students submit an annotated bibliography (with brief notes on each source in their own words) one week before the essay. The bibliography must precede the essay — this creates a sequenced artifact trail.

2. **Transfer element:** The essay prompt is refined to include a case the teacher introduces the week before the due date — a specific recent event or study — and students must connect at least one section of their argument to this new material.

3. **Oral check:** In the week after submission, the teacher holds brief (five-minute) check-ins with students during office hours. One question: "Tell me about the source you found most useful and what you took from it." Students who read and engaged with their sources can answer this. Students who submitted without reading cannot.

The revised design is not foolproof. Nothing is. But it creates multiple points of evidence, not one. It makes AI substitution visible in at least two of three components. And it rewards students who did the learning work.

**Prompt the teacher used with Claude to help redesign:**

> I have a take-home essay assignment that is vulnerable to AI substitution. My learning outcome is: students apply sociological theory to analyze a contemporary media phenomenon and construct an original argument from literature. Help me redesign this into a three-stage assessment with a process component, a transfer element introduced late, and a brief oral check. Do not change the outcome — change the evidence design.

## On Detection and Surveillance

This chapter does not recommend AI detection tools as a primary response to generative AI. The reasons are practical and principled.

Practically: current AI detection tools produce false positives — they flag authentic student work as AI-generated — at rates that are unacceptable for high-stakes decisions (U.S. Department of Education AI report). The tools are also easy to circumvent and will remain so as AI writing becomes more varied. Detection-based responses build an adversarial relationship between teacher and student without solving the underlying assessment design problem.

Principally: the purpose of assessment is to produce evidence of learning, not to police behavior. An assessment that requires detection to function is an assessment that has not been designed to produce its own evidence. Redesign produces evidence. Detection responds to the absence of it.

This does not mean that academic integrity policy is irrelevant. It means that policy should operate at the institutional level — defining what constitutes academic misconduct, establishing processes for review, and specifying consequences — while individual teachers operate at the design level, creating assessments that produce valid evidence. These are different responsibilities.

If you suspect a student's work was produced by AI without engagement, the appropriate first step is a conversation, not a detection tool. Ask the student to explain their work. Their ability to do so is evidence. Their inability is also evidence. That conversation is what teachers have always done when they suspected a student's work did not represent their understanding.

## A Note on Equity

Surveillance-heavy assessment responses to AI — mandatory keystroke logging, continuous camera monitoring, algorithmic flagging — impose burdens unevenly. Students who lack reliable internet access, students with test anxiety, students who are first-generation and already operating under high scrutiny, and students with disabilities are disproportionately affected by monitoring regimes. The burden lands on students who are often already at the margins, not on the AI systems that prompted the response.

Assessment redesign distributes burden more equitably: everyone does the process work, everyone participates in the oral component, everyone submits the annotated bibliography. The design protects the validity of the assessment for all students without surveilling any of them.

## The Human Gate

Before implementing any assessment change, the teacher review questions:

1. **What learning is this assessment measuring?** State it in one sentence. If you cannot, the assessment is not ready.

2. **What can Claude produce that would pass this assessment without the student understanding the material?** Be honest. This is a diagnostic, not an accusation.

3. **What design feature creates evidence of student thinking?** If the answer is "the student promised not to use AI," that is not a design feature.

4. **Is the AI-use boundary teachable?** Students need to understand clearly what is permitted, what requires disclosure, and what is prohibited — and those rules need to be consistent with the course's learning goals.

5. **Have equity implications been considered?** Does this design penalize students with unreliable access to technology, students who learn English as an additional language, or students whose prior education did not include the discourse norms the rubric assumes?

6. **Does this design require detection to function?** If yes, redesign until it does not.

## Common Mistakes

**Reacting to AI with prohibition only.** Saying "no AI tools" is a policy statement, not an assessment design. It may be the right policy for some assessments, but it does not change what the assessment measures or how it produces evidence.

**Ignoring the equity dimension of surveillance.** AI detection tools and monitoring software are not neutral. Before implementing them, ask who bears the burden and whether the assessment design could instead produce the evidence without surveillance.

**Redesigning so much that students stop learning from assessment.** Process portfolios and oral checks take time — from teachers and from students. Do not over-engineer. Choose one or two design patterns, apply them to your highest-stakes assessment, and evaluate. Brookhart's research on assessment and feedback emphasizes that the relationship between assessment and learning depends on feedback loops the student can actually use (Brookhart, assessment and feedback).

**Treating all AI use as equivalent.** A student who uses Claude to check the clarity of a thesis statement is doing something categorically different from a student who submits Claude's thesis statement as their own. Assessment design and disclosure requirements should reflect this range.

**Waiting for institutional policy before acting.** Your institution will eventually clarify its AI policy. That process may take time. Meanwhile, your assessment design is within your control. Start with design.

## Try This

**Exercise 1 (Immediate — vulnerability map):** Take your three most significant course assessments. Apply the vulnerability map from this chapter. For each one, identify the single design change that would produce the most additional evidence of student thinking. You do not need to change everything — find the high-leverage point.

**Exercise 2 (Classroom-applicable — disclosure requirement):** Draft an AI-use disclosure requirement for one assignment. Use this structure: (a) report what tool you used and for what purpose; (b) describe one decision you made to change or reject the AI's output; (c) reflect on how your use affected your learning. Pilot it with one class. What the student reflections reveal about their AI use is data you would not otherwise have.

**Exercise 3 (Redesign):** Take one take-home assignment that concerns you. Use the five design patterns as a menu. Add one process component (a dated brainstorm or outline due before the draft), one transfer element (a new case introduced one week before the deadline), and one oral check (five minutes during office hours). Write the revised assignment prompt. Then use Claude to look for gaps in your redesign:

> Here is my revised assessment design. My learning outcome is [X]. Point out any design elements that Claude could still substitute for student thinking without the student engaging with the material.

Review what Claude identifies. Revise where necessary.

## What Would Change My Mind

This chapter argues that assessment redesign — not detection, not blanket prohibition — is the most productive and equitable response to generative AI in education. That argument would be weakened by evidence that:

- Process-based assessment components are routinely gamed in ways that undermine their validity (students fabricating draft histories, for instance) and that teachers cannot distinguish fabricated process from genuine process
- AI-use disclosure requirements suppress honest reporting and produce false compliance without genuine reflection
- The equity argument against surveillance tools is outweighed by equity arguments for them — if AI use correlates with socioeconomic advantage, detection tools may level a playing field they are accused of tilting

The chapter's underlying position is that valid assessment and student cognitive labor are the things worth protecting, and that redesign serves both better than detection. Evidence that redesign reliably fails to protect either would require reconsidering that position.

## Still Puzzling

**Where is the line between AI-assisted and AI-authored?** Disclosure requirements name this question but do not fully answer it. A student who used Claude to restructure three paragraphs, checked the logic, and then revised the conclusions — is that AI-assisted or AI-authored? The field does not have a settled vocabulary for this range, and the lack of vocabulary makes disclosure requirements ambiguous to implement.

**What happens to the weaker students?** The five design patterns protect assessment validity for the class as a whole. But a student who has been using AI as a substitute for understanding, and who now faces an oral check or a transfer task, is in a harder position than a student who learned the material. The honest answer is: that student's difficulty reveals a learning problem that the previous design was hiding. Revealing it is good for the student in the long run. It may not feel good in the short term.

**Whether redesign is sustainable at scale.** A faculty member teaching four sections of a first-year course with enrollment of thirty students per section is being asked, by this chapter, to conduct brief oral checks with 120 students. The math is real. The chapter does not pretend otherwise. Sustainability depends on institutional support for smaller class sizes, shared assessment tools, and professional development time that most institutions do not currently provide.
