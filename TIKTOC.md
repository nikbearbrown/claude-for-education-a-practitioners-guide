# Part 1 - Book Concept and Thesis

## Book Concept Summary

This book teaches educators how to use Claude to improve planning, feedback, differentiation, research, and student support without outsourcing the teacher's judgment or the student's learning. It fills the gap between generic AI-for-teachers prompt lists and technical Claude books by focusing on classroom decisions, learning outcomes, privacy, assessment integrity, and human responsibility.

## One-Sentence Logline

Claude can help teachers prepare, respond, and adapt, but it cannot decide what learning is for or whether a student has truly learned.

## Central Thesis

This book argues that Claude belongs in education as a professional thinking partner and workflow assistant, not as a replacement teacher, automatic grader, or student shortcut. The teacher's work is to define learning outcomes, protect cognitive struggle, adapt support, verify outputs, and preserve the human relationship that makes education possible.

## Thesis Test

- Act One grounds Claude use in learning outcomes and teacher judgment.
- Act Two teaches practical workflows for planning, feedback, differentiation, assessment, and materials.
- Act Three addresses student use, policy, privacy, equity, and course-level adoption.

**Thesis test:** Pass.

# Part 2 - Learner Profile

## Primary Reader

A teacher, instructional designer, faculty member, TA, or education leader who has tried Claude or ChatGPT but wants a disciplined way to use it in real teaching. They need practical workflows, not hype, and they are concerned about cheating, privacy, quality, and whether students are actually learning.

## Prior Knowledge Assumed

- Experience teaching or supporting learners
- Basic familiarity with learning objectives, assignments, and feedback
- Basic AI-chat experience or willingness to learn

## Prior Knowledge Not Assumed

- Prompt engineering terminology
- Claude Code
- Educational data science
- AI policy expertise
- Software development

## Prior Misconceptions

1. "AI for education means faster lesson plans" - planning speed matters only if the design improves learning.
2. "Claude can grade because it can comment" - feedback and evaluation are not the same.
3. "Students using Claude means learning is impossible" - the issue is workflow design, assessment design, and cognitive labor.
4. "Prompt lists are enough" - prompts without learning outcomes become decoration.

## Motivation Type

**Primary:** Professional.  
**Secondary:** Ethical and instructional.  
**Not primary:** Technical AI development.

# Part 3 - Book Type and Deployment Specification

## Book Type

**Primary type:** Practitioner handbook for educators.  
**Secondary type:** Professional-development course text.  
**Not:** A general AI literacy book, a ban-AI manifesto, or a technical Claude manual.

## Primary Adoption Context

Teacher professional development, university teaching centers, instructional design teams, faculty workshops, and self-directed educators.

## Secondary Adoption Context

Education courses, learning engineering programs, and AI-in-education seminars that need practical classroom workflow examples.

## What This Book Is Not Designed For

- Replacing institutional AI policy
- Automating high-stakes grading
- Teaching students to bypass learning
- Technical Claude Code instruction

# Part 4 - Field Positioning

## Comparable Texts and Gaps

**AI prompt books for teachers** give examples but often lack learning theory and verification discipline.  
**AI policy guides** discuss risk but rarely show day-to-day workflows.  
**Claude ecosystem guides** explain products but not pedagogy.  
This book puts the teacher's instructional judgment at the center.

## Positioning Statement

Unlike generic AI-for-teachers resources that treat Claude as a content generator, this book teaches Claude as a supervised teaching-workflow partner anchored in learning outcomes, feedback quality, assessment integrity, and student cognitive labor.

# Part 5 - Three-Act Learning Arc

## Act One - Protect the Learning

The reader learns to identify which parts of teaching can be assisted and which must remain human: goals, judgment, relationship, and assessment meaning.

## Act Two - Build the Teacher Workflows

The reader practices Claude-supported lesson planning, feedback, differentiation, materials design, rubrics, and reflection.

## Act Three - Design the Classroom System

The reader addresses student use, assessment redesign, privacy, policy, equity, and sustainable adoption.

## Arc Statement

This book takes the reader from anxious or ad hoc Claude use to principled educational workflow design by first protecting learning outcomes, then building teacher-facing workflows, then designing classroom and course policies.

# Part 7 - Chapter-by-Chapter TOC

## Introduction - The Teacher Is Still the System

**Capability built:** Frame Claude as support for teaching judgment, not a substitute for it.

The introduction names the core tension: Claude can make educational artifacts easier to produce, but artifacts are not learning.

## Chapter 1 - What Claude Can and Cannot Do in Education

**Capability built:** Classify teaching tasks by what can be assisted, delegated, or protected.

The chapter distinguishes preparation, explanation, feedback drafting, materials generation, student support, grading, policy, and relational judgment.

## Chapter 2 - Learning Outcomes Before Prompts

**Capability built:** Write Claude requests that begin from learning outcomes.

The reader learns to define what students should be able to do, then ask Claude for activities, examples, and supports aligned to that outcome.

## Chapter 3 - Prompting Claude Like an Instructional Designer

**Capability built:** Turn an educational goal into a structured Claude brief.

Core elements: learner profile, prior knowledge, misconception, outcome, constraints, modality, assessment evidence, and tone.

## Chapter 4 - Lesson Planning and Activity Design

**Capability built:** Use Claude to generate and critique lesson plans without accepting generic plans.

The chapter teaches backward design, timeboxing, active learning, examples, and teacher review.

## Chapter 5 - Feedback That Teaches

**Capability built:** Use Claude to draft feedback that preserves student agency.

The reader learns to generate feedback options, calibrate tone, avoid overhelping, and distinguish coaching from answer-giving.

## Chapter 6 - Differentiation and Accessibility

**Capability built:** Adapt materials for varied learners without lowering the intellectual target.

Topics include reading levels, UDL, multilingual support, scaffolds, examples, and extension tasks.

## Chapter 7 - Rubrics, Exemplars, and Criteria

**Capability built:** Use Claude to clarify expectations and improve assessment transparency.

The chapter teaches rubric drafting, exemplar generation, common-failure examples, and rubric-audit questions.

## Chapter 8 - Assessment in the Age of Claude

**Capability built:** Redesign assessments so they measure learning rather than artifact production.

The chapter covers oral defense, process evidence, in-class work, transfer tasks, reflection, and AI-use disclosure.

## Chapter 9 - Privacy, Student Data, and Institutional Boundaries

**Capability built:** Decide what student information should not be put into Claude.

The reader learns data minimization, anonymization, policy checks, FERPA-like caution, and local institutional rules.

## Chapter 10 - Student Use: From Ban to Boundary

**Capability built:** Write student-facing AI-use rules that are teachable.

The chapter provides policy patterns: prohibited, permitted-with-disclosure, required-for-process, and reflective-use modes.

## Chapter 11 - Research, Advising, and Faculty Workflows

**Capability built:** Use Claude for faculty work beyond lesson planning.

Examples include literature leads, grant outlines, advising notes, assignment redesign, and manuscript feedback, all with verification.

## Chapter 12 - Capstone: A Claude-Supported Unit

**Capability built:** Design a complete unit or module using Claude with human review gates.

The reader produces outcomes, lesson sequence, materials, feedback plan, assessment, AI-use policy, and verification checklist.

# Part 10 - Hard Topics and Aging Risk

## Hard Chapters

- **Chapter 5:** Feedback must not become ghostwriting or grading automation.
- **Chapter 8:** Assessment redesign is pedagogically hard and politically sensitive.
- **Chapter 9:** Privacy guidance must be conservative without claiming to replace policy.

## Aging Risk

High-aging-risk content: Claude interface, school policies, institutional tool access.  
Stable content: learning outcomes, feedback quality, assessment validity, privacy minimization, and teacher judgment.
