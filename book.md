# Claude for Education: A Practitioner's Guide

**Author:** Nik Bear Brown  
**Publisher:** Bear Brown LLC

---

## One-Sentence Pitch

Claude can help teachers prepare, respond, and adapt, but it cannot decide what learning is for or whether a student has truly learned.

## The Argument

<!-- What does this book claim that isn't already obvious or settled?
     What changes in the reader's head between page one and the end?
     2–4 paragraphs. -->

This book argues that Claude belongs in education as a professional thinking partner and workflow assistant, not as a replacement teacher, automatic grader, or student shortcut. The teacher's work is to define learning outcomes, protect cognitive struggle, adapt support, verify outputs, and preserve the human relationship that makes education possible.

The field has not lacked for AI enthusiasm. What it lacks is a disciplined method. Generic prompt lists circulate without connecting to learning theory. Policy guides discuss risk without showing day-to-day workflows. Technical Claude documentation explains the product without explaining pedagogy. This book puts the teacher's instructional judgment at the center and works outward from there.

By the end of this book, readers will be able to classify teaching tasks by what can be assisted and what must remain human, build Claude-supported workflows anchored in learning outcomes, design assessments that measure learning rather than artifact production, and write student-facing AI-use policies that are teachable and defensible.

## The Gap

<!-- Why does this book need to exist? What does it do that no other
     book in the field already does? Name 2–3 books in the same space
     and say briefly how yours differs. -->

AI prompt books for teachers give examples but lack learning theory and verification discipline. AI policy guides discuss risk but rarely show day-to-day workflows. Claude ecosystem guides explain products but not pedagogy.

Unlike generic AI-for-teachers resources that treat Claude as a content generator, this book teaches Claude as a supervised teaching-workflow partner anchored in learning outcomes, feedback quality, assessment integrity, and student cognitive labor. It is the book between the prompt list and the policy document — practical, principled, and honest about what the machine cannot do.

## The Reader

<!-- Who is this book FOR? Be specific — not "anyone interested in X."
     What do they already know? What are they trying to do?
     What will they be able to do after reading it? -->

The primary reader is a teacher, instructional designer, faculty member, TA, or education leader who has tried Claude or ChatGPT but wants a disciplined way to use it in real teaching. They have experience with learning objectives, assignments, and feedback. They are concerned about cheating, privacy, quality, and whether students are actually learning.

This reader does not need prompt engineering terminology, Claude Code, educational data science, AI policy expertise, or software development background. They need practical workflows, not hype, and they are ready to think carefully about which parts of their work should stay human.

## High-Level Outline

<!-- Three to five acts / parts / movements. Not chapters yet — those
     live in outline.md. This is the shape of the argument at altitude. -->

**Act One — Protect the Learning**  
The reader learns to identify which parts of teaching can be assisted and which must remain human: goals, judgment, relationship, and assessment meaning. Covers the Introduction and Chapters 1–3.

**Act Two — Build the Teacher Workflows**  
The reader practices Claude-supported lesson planning, feedback, differentiation, materials design, rubrics, and reflection. Covers Chapters 4–7.

**Act Three — Design the Classroom System**  
The reader addresses student use, assessment redesign, privacy, policy, equity, and sustainable adoption. Covers Chapters 8–12.
